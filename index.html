CASE 01Independent projectE-commerce · Mobile
EXHIBIT AOFFSIDE / MOBILE

Offside: giving fan gear a stadium-night feel.

Role — UI/UX Designer (solo) · Tools — Figma

Problem

Most sports merchandise apps read like generic catalogues — clean grids that could be selling anything. They lose the emotional pull that makes fans want to buy in the first place.

Approach

A moody, neon-on-black visual system borrowed from stadium floodlighting and pitch-side branding — kept deliberately simple underneath: filtering, sizing, checkout.

Highlights

  • Floodlit product photography treatment that carries stadium energy onto the product page itself.
  • Persistent bottom navigation with quick category pills — Jerseys, Boots, Training — so browsing never loses momentum.
  • A fully itemised cart: size, quantity, shipping destination, promo code and total, so nothing is a surprise before checkout.

CASE 02Independent projectHealthcare · Web
EXHIBIT BCLINICOS / WEB

ClinicOS: one system, three very different jobs.

Role — UI/UX Designer (solo) · Tools — Figma

Problem

A single clinic visit touches three jobs — the admin booking the slot, the doctor reading the chart, the front desk closing out billing — and most clinic software forces all three into the same generic table.

Approach

One consistent design system — colour coding, typography, component library — that adapts to what each role needs first: appointments for admin, vitals for a physician, an itemised invoice at discharge.

Highlights

  • Colour-coded appointment statuses — confirmed, checked-in, in-consultation, cancelled — so a full day is scannable at a glance.
  • A vitals-first patient profile: BP, heart rate, temperature, O2, BMI, above the medical history timeline.
  • A discharge summary that reconciles prescriptions, procedures and insurance coverage into one payable total.

CASE 03Independent projectTravel · Web
EXHIBIT CSTAYNEST / WEB

StayNest: booking a stay you can trust.

Role — UI/UX Designer (solo) · Tools — Figma

Problem

A luxury booking is a high-value decision. Uncertainty at any step — an unclear price breakdown, a vague cancellation policy, no sense of what comes after paying — quietly erodes trust before checkout.

Approach

One calm, indigo-on-white language carried from homepage search through hotel detail into checkout, with the price breakdown shown at every step instead of only at the end.

Highlights

  • A persistent search summary bar that keeps destination, dates and guest count visible while filtering.
  • A "why guests love this" module on the hotel page that builds confidence before price is discussed.
  • A post-booking confirmation that pre-answers the next question: check-in time, digital check-in, cancellation window.

CASE 04Independent projectHR Tech · Web
EXHIBIT DTALENTFLOW / WEB

TalentFlow: the hiring funnel, made visible.

Role — UI/UX Designer (solo) · Tools — Figma

Problem

Recruiters often track candidates across scattered spreadsheets and inboxes, making it hard to see where a hire is actually stuck — or why.

Approach

The entire hiring funnel modelled as one kanban-style pipeline, paired with a dashboard of numbers a recruiting lead checks weekly, and a single-scroll profile per candidate instead of scattered documents.

Highlights

  • A pipeline board moving candidates through Applied → Screening → Interview → Assessment → Offer → Hired at a glance.
  • A weighted evaluation scorecard — communication, technical skill, problem solving, cultural fit — in every profile.
  • Interview scheduling shown as a weekly calendar grid instead of a flat list, so clashes are obvious before they happen.

DRAWER 02 — METHOD

A process that stays the same, even when the problem doesn't.

Four stages, always in this order — skipping research or testing is where most redesigns quietly fail.

01 / DISCOVER

Understand before designing.

Look at the existing flow, the stakeholders involved, and where the real friction lives — not where it's assumed to live.

Competitive auditUser flowsProblem framing
02 / DEFINE

Turn findings into a brief.

Map the journey, define the information architecture, and decide what "done" looks like before a single high-fidelity screen is drawn.

Journey mapsWireframesIA
03 / DESIGN

Systemize as you go.

Build in components from day one in Figma, so what ships is consistent, accessible, and easy for engineering to implement.

High-fidelity UIPrototypingDesign systems
04 / DELIVER

Hand off and iterate.

Collaborate closely with developers through build, and revisit screens as feedback and new requirements come in.

Dev handoffFeedback loopsIteration

CASE 01Independent projectE-commerce · Mobile

Offside: giving fan gear a stadium-night feel.

Role: UI/UX Designer (solo) · Tools: Figma

Problem

Most sports merchandise apps read like generic catalogues — clean grids that could be selling anything. They lose the emotional pull that makes fans want to buy in the first place.

Approach

Built a moody, neon-on-black visual system that borrows from stadium floodlighting and pitch-side branding, then kept the actual shopping mechanics — filtering, sizing, checkout — deliberately simple underneath the mood.

Highlights

  • Bold, floodlit product photography treatment that carries the sports energy onto the product page itself.
  • Persistent bottom navigation and quick category pills (Jerseys, Boots, Training) so browsing never loses momentum.
  • A fully itemised cart — size, quantity, shipping destination, promo code, and total — so nothing about the order is a surprise before checkout.

CASE 02Independent projectHealthcare · Web

ClinicOS: one system, three very different jobs.

Role: UI/UX Designer (solo) · Tools: Figma

Problem

A single clinic visit touches three different jobs — the admin booking the slot, the doctor reading the chart, the front desk closing out billing — and most clinic software forces all three into the same generic table view.

Approach

Designed one consistent design system — colour coding, typography, component library — and let the layout adapt to what each role actually needs to see first: appointments for admin, vitals and history for a physician, an itemised invoice at discharge.

Highlights

  • Colour-coded appointment statuses (confirmed, checked-in, in-consultation, cancelled) so a full day is scannable at a glance.
  • A vitals-first patient profile — BP, heart rate, temperature, O2, BMI — surfaced above the medical history timeline.
  • A discharge summary that reconciles prescriptions, procedures, and insurance coverage into one payable total.

CASE 03Independent projectTravel · Web

StayNest: booking a stay you can trust.

Role: UI/UX Designer (solo) · Tools: Figma

Problem

A luxury booking is a high-value decision. Any uncertainty along the way — an unclear price breakdown, a vague cancellation policy, no sense of what happens after paying — quietly erodes trust before checkout.

Approach

Carried one calm, indigo-on-white visual language from homepage search through hotel detail and into checkout, with the price breakdown shown at every single step instead of only at the end.

Highlights

  • A persistent search summary bar that keeps destination, dates and guest count visible while filtering results.
  • A "why guests love this" module on the hotel page that builds confidence before the price is even discussed.
  • A post-booking confirmation that pre-answers the next three questions a guest will have: check-in time, digital check-in, and the cancellation window.

CASE 04Independent projectHR Tech · Web

TalentFlow: the hiring funnel, made visible.

Role: UI/UX Designer (solo) · Tools: Figma

Problem

Recruiters often track candidates across scattered spreadsheets and inboxes, which makes it hard to see where a hire is actually stuck in the process — or why.

Approach

Modelled the entire hiring funnel as one kanban-style pipeline, paired it with a dashboard of the numbers a recruiting lead checks weekly, and gave every candidate a single-scroll profile instead of scattered documents.

Highlights

  • A pipeline board that moves candidates through Applied → Screening → Interview → Assessment → Offer → Hired at a glance.
  • A weighted evaluation scorecard — communication, technical skill, problem solving, cultural fit — built into every candidate profile.
  • Interview scheduling shown as a weekly calendar grid instead of a flat list, so clashes are obvious before they happen.

FIG. 03 — HOW SHE WORKS

A process that stays the same, even when the problem doesn't.

Four stages, always in this order — because skipping research or testing is where most redesigns quietly fail.

01 / DISCOVER

Understand before designing.

Look at the existing flow, the stakeholders involved, and where the real friction lives — not where it's assumed to live.

Competitive auditUser flowsProblem framing
02 / DEFINE

Turn findings into a brief.

Map the journey, define the information architecture, and decide what "done" looks like before a single high-fidelity screen is drawn.

Journey mapsWireframesIA
03 / DESIGN

Systemize as you go.

Build in components from day one in Figma, so what ships is consistent, accessible, and easy for engineering to implement.

High-fidelity UIPrototypingDesign systems
04 / DELIVER

Hand off and iterate.

Collaborate closely with developers through build, and revisit screens as feedback and new requirements come in.

Dev handoffFeedback loopsIteration
